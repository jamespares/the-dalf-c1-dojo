## PART 1: EXAM SPECIFICATIONS & BLUEPRINT

### 1.1 Overview & Key Facts

The **DALF C1** (Diplome Approfondi de Langue Francaise — Niveau C1) is an official diploma issued by the **French Ministry of National Education** (Ministere de l'Education nationale) on behalf of **France Education International** (officially CIEP).

| Feature | DALF C1 Specification |
|---------|----------------------|
| **Issuing body** | France Education International (French Ministry of Education) |
| **CEFR level** | C1 (Proficient User — Experienced/Autonomous) |
| **Diploma validity** | Lifetime — no expiration |
| **Target audience** | Adults (18+), foreign and French nationals |
| **Academic equivalence** | Required for French master's programs and grandes ecoles |
| **International recognition** | Worldwide recognition by universities, employers, immigration bodies |
| **Exam frequency** | 2–4 sessions per year depending on center |
| **Total duration** | 5h30 (4h collective + 1h30 oral with prep) |
| **Total score** | /100 |
| **Pass mark** | 50/100 |
| **Minimum per section** | 5/25 (elimination threshold) |
| **Number of sections** | 4 (Listening, Reading, Writing, Speaking) |
| **Exam format** | 3 collective (group) + 1 individual |
| **Prerequisites** | None (direct registration possible) |
| **Waiting period between sittings** | 45 days minimum |

### 1.2 The Four Sections (Detailed)

| Section | French Name | Skill Type | Nature |
|---------|-------------|------------|--------|
| 1 | **Comprehension de l'oral** | Listening (Receptive) | Collective exam |
| 2 | **Comprehension des ecrits** | Reading (Receptive) | Collective exam |
| 3 | **Production ecrite** | Writing (Productive) | Collective exam |
| 4 | **Production orale** | Speaking (Productive) | Individual exam |

#### Section 1: Comprehension de l'oral (Listening Comprehension)
- **Score:** /25 points
- **Two exercises:**
  - **Exercise 1 (Long document):** One long audio recording (~8 minutes, e.g., interview, course, lecture, conference) played **twice**
  - **Exercise 2 (Short documents):** Several brief radio-style documents (news flashes, polls, advertisements) played **once only**
- **Maximum audio duration:** 10 minutes total
- **Question types:** Comprehension questionnaires answered during/after listening

#### Section 2: Comprehension des ecrits (Reading Comprehension)
- **Score:** /25 points
- **One exercise:** Reading comprehension questionnaire based on a single long text
- **Text type:** Texte d'idees (literary or journalistic) of 1,500–2,000 words
- **Question types:** Mix of multiple-choice (QCM), true/false, and short written answer questions requiring brief responses
- **Note:** Unlike DELF A2–B2 (which moved to MCQ-only in 2020), DALF C1 **retains open-ended/written response questions**

#### Section 3: Production ecrite (Written Production)
- **Score:** /25 points
- **Two mandatory parts** based on a dossier of documents (~1,000 words total):
  - **Part 1 — Synthese (12.5 points):** Objective synthesis of 220–240 words maximum summarizing the key ideas from all documents provided. Must be written in the candidate's own words without personal opinion.
  - **Part 2 — Essai argumente (12.5 points):** Argumentative essay of minimum 250 words expressing a personal opinion on the theme of the dossier, responding to a specific problem statement (problematique). Must be structured with clear arguments.
- **Domains:** Since March 2020, candidates **no longer choose** between "Lettres et sciences humaines" and "Sciences" — all candidates take the same exam

#### Section 4: Production orale (Speaking Production)
- **Score:** /25 points
- **Preparation time:** 1 hour (candidate receives a dossier of documents to prepare)
- **Exam duration:** 30 minutes
- **Format — Two parts:**
  - **Part 1 — Expose (Monologue):** 10-minute structured presentation based on the documents received during preparation. Must summarize key issues and present a personal perspective with arguments.
  - **Part 2 — Discussion (Interaction):** 20-minute debate/discussion with the two examiners who ask questions, challenge viewpoints, and deepen the exchange on the theme.
- **Exam format:** Brief informal chat -> 10-min presentation -> discussion with jury

| Component | Total Time |
|-----------|------------|
| **Collective exams** | **4 hours** |
| **Individual exam (including prep)** | **1 hour 30 minutes** |
| **Grand total** | **5 hours 30 minutes** |

**Important timing notes:**
- The collective exam time is **global** — candidates manage their own time between reading and answering
- For the long listening document: 3 min reading questions -> 1st listen -> 3 min pause -> 2nd listen -> 5 min to complete answers
- The short documents are heard **once only** — candidates must be attentive from the start
- For each short document: 50 seconds to read questions -> listen -> 50 seconds to answer

### 1.4 Scoring System & Pass Requirements

#### Score Breakdown

| Component | Maximum Score | Sub-breakdown |
|-----------|--------------|---------------|
| Comprehension de l'oral | /25 | Long document ~18 pts + Short docs ~7 pts |
| Comprehension des ecrits | /25 | Various question types |
| Production ecrite | /25 | Synthese 12.5 pts + Essai argumente 12.5 pts |
| Production orale | /25 | Expose + Entretien + Language |
| **TOTAL** | **/100** | |

#### Pass Requirements

| Requirement | Minimum Score |
|-------------|--------------|
| **Overall pass mark** | **50/100** |
| **Minimum per section** | **5/25 per skill** |

**Critical Rules:**
- A score **below 5/25 in any single section is eliminatory** — the candidate fails the entire exam regardless of their total score
- Example: A candidate scoring 60/100 total but only 3/25 in Writing **will NOT pass**
- Each section is independent; skills are assessed separately

#### Results Timeline
- **Attestation of success (temporary marksheet):** Issued within approximately 2 months
- **Official diploma:** Issued by the French Ministry of Education, usually arrives 3–6 months after the exam session

### 1.5 CEFR C1 Level Descriptors

The DALF C1 corresponds to the **"Proficient User — Experienced User (Autonomous)"** category of the Common European Framework of Reference for Languages.

#### General Competence Statements

| Skill | Official CEFR C1 Descriptor | What It Means for DALF C1 |
|-------|---------------------------|---------------------------|
| **Overall** | "Can understand a wide range of demanding, longer texts, and recognise implicit meaning." | Must grasp not just explicit content but also implicit meanings, irony, cultural references, and subtext in complex texts and audio |
| **Overall** | "Can express ideas fluently and spontaneously without much obvious searching for expressions." | Must demonstrate effortless communication without visible hesitation or word-searching |
| **Overall** | "Can use language flexibly and effectively for social, academic and professional purposes." | Must adapt language register to different contexts (formal, academic, professional) |
| **Listening** | "Can understand extended speech even when it is not clearly structured and when relationships are only implied and not signalled explicitly." | Must follow unstructured arguments, lectures, and debates where logical connectors may be absent |
| **Reading** | "Can understand in detail long, complex factual and literary texts, appreciating distinctions of style." | Must analyze literary and journalistic texts for tone, style, implicit meaning, and argumentative structure |
| **Writing** | "Can express themselves in clear, well-structured text, expressing points of view at some length." | Must produce well-organized synthesis and argumentative texts with coherent paragraph structure |
| **Speaking** | "Can present a complex topic confidently and articulately, structuring and adapting the talk flexibly to the audience." | Must deliver a structured 10-minute presentation and handle an interactive debate with examiners |

#### Global C1 Descriptor (Official)

> "Can understand a wide range of demanding, longer texts, and recognise implicit meaning. Can express ideas fluently and spontaneously without much obvious searching for expressions. Can use language flexibly and effectively for social, academic and professional purposes. Can produce clear, well-structured, detailed text on complex subjects, showing controlled use of organisational patterns, connectors and cohesive devices."

#### The "C1 Candidate" Profile

A DALF C1 candidate should be able to:
- **Understand** a wide range of long and demanding texts and grasp implicit meanings
- **Express themselves** spontaneously and fluently without too much apparent searching for words
- **Use language** effectively and flexibly in social, professional, and academic contexts
- **Express themselves** on complex topics in a clear, well-structured manner and demonstrate control of organizational, articulatory, and discourse cohesion tools
- **Master implicit meaning:** Understand non-explicit content, irony, and cultural references in long complex texts
- **Precision:** Choose the right word to express a nuanced opinion even on abstract or technical subjects
- **Organization:** Structure discourse with varied logical connectors and smooth transitions between ideas

#### Difference Between B2 and C1

| Dimension | B2 Level | C1 Level |
|-----------|---------|---------|
| Text length | ~1,000 words | ~1,500-2,000 words |
| Argumentation | Defends opinion with examples | Problematizes, nuance, synthesizes |
| Vocabulary | Good range, some searching | Rich, precise, overcomes gaps with ease |
| Grammar | Good control, some errors | High accuracy, complex varied structures |
| Register | Generally appropriate | Flexible adaptation to recipient |
| Cohesion | Clear organization | Sophisticated connectors and transitions |

- **B2** = Functional adequacy: can communicate effectively in most situations
- **C1** = Effortless precision: can use language with sophistication, understand implicit content, and produce structured intellectual discourse

### 1.6 Post-2020 Exam Format Changes

#### Major Change: March 2020 — Elimination of Specialization Domains

**Before March 2020:**
- DALF C1 candidates chose between two domains:
  - **Lettres et sciences humaines** (Arts and Humanities)
  - **Sciences** (Sciences)
- The written and oral production exams used documents specific to the chosen domain
- ~80% of candidates chose the LSH domain

**After March 2020:**
- **Domain choice eliminated** — all candidates now take the same unified exam
- Documents are drawn from **all subject areas** to assess the candidate's ability to handle any complex topic regardless of specialization
- Themes are chosen to be **accessible to all candidates** regardless of academic background

**Rationale:** France Education International determined that the linguistic skills required for both domains were essentially identical, and the domain choice created unnecessary confusion.

#### What Did NOT Change for DALF C1 in 2020

| Element | Status |
|---------|--------|
| 4-section exam structure | Unchanged |
| Exam durations | Unchanged |
| Scoring system (/100, min 5/25 per section) | Unchanged |
| Open-ended questions (unlike DELF A2–B2 which moved to MCQ-only) | Retained |
| Synthesis + argumentative essay format | Unchanged |
| Listening documents (long + short) format | Unchanged |
| Oral exam format (prep + presentation + discussion) | Unchanged |

#### Summary: DELF vs. DALF Changes

| Level | Changes Implemented |
|-------|-------------------|
| **DELF A2–B2** | Major format changes (2020–2024): more documents, MCQ-only in comprehension, adjusted durations |
| **DELF A1** | Changes delayed to late 2021 |
| **DALF C1 & C2** | Only the domain specialization was removed (March 2020); core structure unchanged |

#### New Evaluation Grids (2021–2022)
- New evaluation grids for production exams (written and oral) were experimented with in 2021
- These grids were designed to improve scoring reliability and validity
- They were progressively implemented from September 2022
- These grids apply to all DELF-DALF levels (including C1)

### 1.7 Text Types Used Per Section

#### Comprehension de l'oral (Listening)

**Long Document (~8 minutes, heard TWICE):**
- Interviews (entretiens)
- University courses/lectures (cours, conferences)
- In-depth discussions or debates
- Documentary-style presentations

**Short Documents (heard ONCE only):**
- News flashes (flashs d'informations)
- Radio surveys/polls (sondages)
- Advertisements/radio spots (spots publicitaires)
- Brief news bulletins

**Content characteristics:**
- Authentic radio broadcasts and recordings
- Native speaker speed and pronunciation
- Various accents and registers
- Complex, nuanced discourse with implicit meaning

#### Comprehension des ecrits (Reading)

- **One single long text** of 1,500–2,000 words
- **Text type:** Texte d'idees — either:
  - **Literary text:** Excerpts from novels, essays, literary criticism
  - **Journalistic text:** Opinion pieces, feature articles, editorials from serious press
- **Content level:** Demanding, requiring analysis of implicit meaning, tone, irony, and argumentative structure
- **Theme:** Drawn from current societal issues accessible to all candidates (since 2020 reform)

#### Production ecrite (Writing)

**Input materials (dossier):**
- 2–3 documents totaling ~1,000 words on a common theme
- Documents may include: press articles, reports, opinion pieces, statistical data, short excerpts

**Output required:**
- **Synthese (220–240 words):** Objective reformulation synthesizing key points from ALL documents
- **Essai argumente (min. 250 words):** Personal argumentative essay responding to a problem statement

#### Production orale (Speaking)

**Input materials (dossier for preparation):**
- Several short written documents on a theme
- Documents may come from various sources: press articles, short texts, data presentations

**Output required:**
- **Expose (10 minutes):** Structured presentation summarizing key issues and presenting a personal analytical perspective
- **Discussion (20 minutes):** Interactive debate with examiners defending the presented viewpoint

### 1.8 Registration & Logistics

#### Eligibility

| Criterion | Requirement |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Age** | Adults (minimum 18 years old) |
| **Nationality** | Open to foreign nationals and French nationals |
| **Prerequisites** | No prerequisite diploma required |
| **Waiting period** | Minimum 45 days between two sittings of the same level |
| **ID requirement** | Must present original identity document for all parts of the exam |

#### Registration Process

1. **Locate an accredited exam center** via the official France Education International exam centre directory or local Alliance Francaise
2. **Check exam calendar** — each center publishes its own session dates (typically 2–4 sessions per year)
3. **Complete online registration** with personal details and desired level
4. **Upload required documents:** Valid ID, photo, and previous candidate number (if retaking)
5. **Pay examination fee** — varies significantly by country
6. **Receive confirmation slip/admit card** before the exam date
7. **Attend exam on scheduled dates** — written and oral exams may be on different days
8. **Collect results** — attestation within ~2 months; diploma within 3–6 months

#### Exam Schedule (Typical Annual Sessions)

- **In France and major centers:** March, June, September/October, December
- **Abroad:** Varies by country (typically March, June, October, December at Alliances Francaises)
- Written and oral exams may be scheduled on **one or two separate days**

#### Exam Day Requirements
- Use **black or blue pen only** (no pencil, except for rough drafts)
- For collective exams, **time is global** — learn to allocate time between reading and writing
- For listening: use the **draft column** on the right side of the questionnaire for notes; only answers in the left column will be graded
- **Reformulate rather than copy** — it's essential to use your own words
- Do **not copy entire sentences** from provided documents

## PART 3: LISTENING COMPREHENSION — FULL DATASET

### 3.1 Exam Structure (Long + Short Documents)

The DALF C1 listening comprehension exam (Comprehension de l'oral) tests the candidate's ability to understand complex spoken French in various contexts. The exam is worth 25 points out of 100 total.

| Component | Description | Duration | Points |
|-----------|-------------|----------|--------|
| **Total listening section** | Both exercises combined | ~40 minutes | /25 |
| **Part 1: Long recording** | 1 document long (interview, course, conference...) ~6-8 minutes, played TWICE | ~18 min + answering time | ~18 points |
| **Part 2: Short recordings** | Several brief radio broadcasts (news flashes, surveys, adverts...), played ONCE | ~10 min max total | ~7 points |

#### Long Document (Exercise 1) Characteristics
- Radio interviews or panel discussions
- 6-8 minutes duration
- Played TWICE
- Mix of scripted narration and spontaneous speech
- Multiple speakers (journalist + expert(s))
- Standard to formal register, may include some colloquial language
- Complex, nuanced discourse with implicit meaning
- Multiple viewpoints on a complex theme

#### Short Documents (Exercise 2) Characteristics
- 2-3 brief extracts
- 20-50 seconds each approximately
- Played ONCE only
- News flashes, surveys, advertisements
- Informative in nature
- Test ability to identify essential information quickly

### 3.2 Timing Protocol

#### Exercise 1 (Long Recording) Timing Breakdown
1. 3 minutes to read questions
2. First listening
3. 3 minutes to begin answering
4. Second listening

## PART 4: MARKING SCHEMES & GRADING RUBRICS

### 4.1 Overall Scoring Framework

| Component | Maximum Score | Sub-breakdown |
|-----------|--------------|---------------|
| Comprehension de l'oral | /25 | Long document ~18 pts + Short docs ~7 pts |
| Comprehension des ecrits | /25 | Various question types (raw /50, divided by 2) |
| Production ecrite | /25 | Synthese 12.5 pts + Essai argumente 12.5 pts |
| Production orale | /25 | Expose 5 pts + Entretien 5 pts + Language 15 pts |
| **TOTAL** | **/100** | |

**Passing threshold:** 50/100 overall, with a minimum of **5/25 per section**.

**Important rule:** A score below 5/25 in ANY section is eliminatory, even if the total score exceeds 50/100.

#### Double Evaluation System

> "La double evaluation est obligatoire pour tous les niveaux et toutes les competences" (Double evaluation is mandatory for all levels and all competencies)

**Process:**
- Every paper is marked by **two independent correctors**
- Correctors must have habilitation (certification) from France Education International
- A **harmonized note** (note harmonisee) is established after discussion if scores diverge
- In case of significant disagreement, a third corrector may be consulted

### 4.2 Listening Comprehension Marking

#### Structure Recap
- **Exercise 1 (long document):** ~8-minute recording, played twice, worth approximately **18-19 points**
- **Exercise 2 (short documents):** Several brief radio broadcasts, played once, worth approximately **6-7 points**
- Maximum recording duration: 10 minutes

#### Question Types and Point Allocation

**Exercise 1 (Long document):**

| Question Type | Points per Question |
|--------------|-------------------|
| MCQ (multiple choice) | 1 point |
| True/False with justification | 1 point |
| Short open answer | 1-2 points |
| Detailed open answer | 1.5-2 points |

**Exercise 2 (Short documents):**

| Document | Points |
|---------|--------|
| Document 1 | 2 points |
| Document 2 | 1 point |
| Document 3 | 3 points (multiple sub-questions) |

#### Key Marking Principles
- Answers must be **reformulated** — copying exact phrases from the audio is penalized
- For open questions: the **justification/substantiation** is what earns marks, not just yes/no or true/false
- MCQs: only the correct answer receives full points; no partial credit
- Incorrect spelling and grammar in responses are **NOT penalized** unless they change the meaning
- Candidates must demonstrate ability to:
  - Grasp general aspects of the text
  - Identify ideas that have been reformulated in the questions (out of order from the audio)
  - Make connections between information in the text
  - Infer implicit meanings and attitudes

#### Scoring Conversion
The raw score (typically out of 25 or 50 points on the answer key) is converted to the final **/25** mark.

### 4.3 Reading Comprehension Marking

#### Structure Recap
- **One text** (literary or journalistic argumentative text), **1,500 to 2,000 words**
- **Duration:** 50 minutes
- Raw score typically out of 50, divided by 2 to obtain final mark /25

#### Question Categories

**A. Questions on the whole text (global comprehension):**
- Identify the author's thesis
- Analyze and compare different perspectives
- Summarize the text's argument in one sentence without using the text's words (3 points)

**B. Detailed comprehension questions:**
- MCQs (1-1.5 points each)
- True/False/Not mentioned with justification
- Short open questions requiring reformulation
- Questions requiring identification of specific arguments or examples

**C. Questions requiring:**
- Reformulation of extracts ("en d'autres termes...")
- Interpretation of implicit meanings
- Analysis of argumentative structure

#### Sample Marking Scheme (from Official Example Subject)

| Question Category | Points |
|------------------|--------|
| Global analysis (schools of literary thought) | 1.5 points |
| Summary comparison table (2 concepts) | 3 points |
| MCQ section A | 1 point |
| Detailed comprehension with justification | 1.5 points |
| Character/vision identification | 1.5 points |
| MCQ section B | 1 point |
| Argument identification | 2 points |
| Textual analysis questions | 1-2 points each |

**Total raw score:** 50 points, divided by 2 to obtain the final mark /25.

#### Key Marking Principles
- **Reformulation is required** — copy-pasting from the text is penalized
- For justification questions: the reasoning earns marks, not just the position
- Open questions: candidates must demonstrate ability to synthesize dispersed information
- Spelling errors are not penalized unless they impede understanding

#### Critical Evaluation Criteria
The candidate must demonstrate:
1. **Global comprehension:** Identify thesis, intention, and text organization
2. **Detailed comprehension:** Locate and reformulate specific information
3. **Inference:** Understand implicit meanings, irony, polemics
4. **Synthesis:** Connect dispersed information and summarize
5. **Reformulation:** Express ideas in own words concisely

### 4.8 Penalty Rules & Anomalies

#### Synthesis Anomalies Summary

| Anomaly | Consequence |
|---------|-------------|
| Hors-sujet thematique | Cannot score C1+ on "realisation de la tache" and "lexique" |
| Hors-sujet discursif | Cannot score C1 or C1+ on "realisation de la tache" and "coherence et cohesion" |
| Hors-sujet complet | 0 on objectivity, realization, and coherence; max 1 on lexique and morphosyntaxe |
| Copie blanche (blank) | 0 on all criteria |
| Manque de matiere (<100 words) | 0 on all criteria |
| Recopiage (>3/4 copied from sources) | 0 on lexique and morphosyntaxe |
| Longueur excessive (300+ words) | 0 on length criterion; max 1 on "realisation de la tache" |

#### General Penalty Rules Across All Sections

| Rule | Application |
|------|-------------|
| **Copy-pasting from sources** | Penalized in comprehension (reformulation required) and in synthesis (recopiage rule) |
| **Spelling/grammar errors** | Not penalized unless they impede understanding (comprehension) or are excessive (production) |
| **Off-topic responses** | Scoring cap applied per anomaly table above |
| **Blank submission** | Zero on all criteria |
| **Insufficient length** | Zero on all criteria if below threshold |

The official examiner manual identifies these biases that can harm scoring:

### 4.10 Common Candidate Errors & Feedback Patterns

#### Synthesis — Most Common Errors

1. **Rule of objectivity violation:** Adding personal opinions or elements not in documents
2. **Juxtaposed summaries:** Writing two separate summaries instead of one integrated synthesis
3. **Following document order:** Not creating a personal organization plan
4. **Length violations:** Writing too few (<200) or too many (>240) words
5. **Copying passages:** Lifting whole sentences from source documents
6. **Missing key information:** Omitting important ideas from one or both documents
7. **Thematic irrelevance:** Identifying wrong common theme

#### Essay — Most Common Errors

1. **Poor structure:** No clear plan or unbalanced parts
2. **Lack of examples:** Arguments remain abstract without concrete illustration
3. **Missing problem statement:** No clear thesis or research question
4. **Register errors:** Using informal language in formal context
5. **No recipient adaptation:** Not considering the intended audience
6. **Weak conclusion:** No synthesis or opening
7. **Repetition:** Same ideas expressed in different forms

#### Speaking — Most Common Errors

1. **Absent or poorly formulated problem statement**
2. **Unbalanced or nonexistent plan**
3. **No time for planning:** Launching into monologue without structure
4. **Introduction too long or incomplete** (missing context, problem, or plan)
5. **No opening in conclusion**
6. **Repetitive arguments** without distinct points per section
7. **Lack of concrete examples**
8. **Poor time management:** Monologue too short or too long
9. **No transitions between parts**
10. **Unclear elocution:** Too fast, monotonous, or poorly articulated
11. **Lack of assurance or interaction with jury**
12. **Excessive use of familiar register**

#### Reading Comprehension — Common Errors

1. **Not clearly identifying author's thesis**
2. **Confusing arguments, examples, and nuances**
3. **Getting stuck on unknown words** instead of following global logic
4. **Poor reformulation:** Copying from text instead of paraphrasing
5. **Inference errors:** Reading too much or too little into the text

#### Listening Comprehension — Common Errors

1. **Not reading questions before listening**
2. **Taking notes that are too detailed** and missing the overall message
3. **Failing to reformulate** and copying from the audio
4. **Not using the 3-minute preparation time** effectively
5. **Spending too much time** on one question and missing subsequent ones

#### Sample Examiner Comments

**Synthesis Example (Score: 8.5/12.5):**

> "Respect de la consigne de longueur (0.5/0.5): With 215 words, the text respects the length constraint."

> "Respect de la regle d'objectivite (0.5/1.5): We can identify 3 infidelities (additions of ideas not in the documents): 'Since the 2000s, robots have invaded every sector of daily life' (no idea of invasion in the documents, but ongoing revolution)..."

> "Realisation de la tache (1.5/2.5): Correct introduction and precisely stated problem thanks to the title. Most essential information well reformulated. Missing 2 important ideas: little evolution since first robots / advent of service robotics with AI."

**Oral Examiner Feedback Pattern:**
- Topic presentation: Too general statements are penalized ("I will talk about biodiversity" -> needs precision)
- Contextualization: Missing contextualization of the topic
- Document presentation: Missing succinct summary of document content
- Problem statement: Needs clearer formulation
- Plan announcement: Needs more structure

### 5.2 Listening Comprehension Generation Rules

#### Exercise 1: Long Document Generation

**Audio Script Requirements:**
- **Duration:** 6-8 minutes (target: ~6 minutes)
- **Speakers:** 2-3 speakers (journalist/interviewer + 1-2 expert interviewees)
- **Format:** Radio interview or documentary discussion
- **Register:** Standard to formal French, with occasional colloquial expressions in spontaneous responses
- **Structure:** Journalist introduction -> Interview questions/responses -> Conclusion
- **Content:** Complex societal theme requiring analysis; multiple viewpoints expressed

**Mandatory Script Elements:**
1. Opening by journalist with context setting (30-60 seconds)
2. At least 4 question-answer exchanges
3. Multiple perspectives or nuanced positions on the theme
4. At least 2 instances of implicit meaning or indirect expression
5. Concrete examples illustrating abstract concepts
6. Natural speech markers (hesitations, reformulations, connectors)

**Question Generation Rules:**

| Question Type | Count | Points | Rules |
|--------------|-------|--------|-------|
| Multiple choice (QCM) | 3-4 | 1 each | 3-4 options; one clearly correct; distractors plausible but incorrect |
| True/False/Not mentioned | 2-3 | 1 each | Requires justification; tests inference ability |
| Short open answer | 2-3 | 1-2 each | Requires reformulation; 1-2 sentence response |
| Table/chart completion | 1 | 2 points | Tests synthesis of dispersed information |
| Detailed open answer | 1 | 2 points | Requires connecting multiple pieces of information |

**Total Exercise 1:** ~18 points

#### Exercise 2: Short Document Generation

**Audio Script Requirements:**
- **Duration:** 20-50 seconds per document (3 documents = ~2 minutes total)
- **Plays:** Once only
- **Types:** News flash, radio survey/poll, advertisement
- **Register:** Informative; advertisements may use persuasive/marketing register

**Mandatory Elements per Document:**
1. Clear main information point (what/who/where/when)
2. One secondary detail requiring attention
3. Implicit information requiring inference
4. Distinct from other documents in the set (different topics)

**Question Generation Rules:**

| Document | Question Type | Points |
|----------|--------------|--------|
| Document 1 (News) | MCQ or short answer | 2 points |
| Document 2 (Survey/Ad) | MCQ | 1-2 points |
| Document 3 (Mixed) | MCQ + short answer | 3 points |

**Total Exercise 2:** ~7 points

#### Timing Specification to Include

For the generated exam paper, include this timing:
- Exercise 1: 3 min read questions -> 1st listen -> 3 min answer -> 2nd listen -> 5 min complete
- Exercise 2 (per document): 50 sec read -> listen -> 50 sec answer

#### Theme Selection for Listening

Select ONE primary theme for the long document from:
- Environment and sustainable development
- Urbanism and city transformation
- Culture and arts (photography, literature, cinema)
- Social issues (family, education, work, solidarity)
- Science and technology
- Economics and society

Select DIFFERENT secondary themes for short documents (never same as long document theme).

### 5.3 Reading Text Generation Rules

#### Text Requirements

| Attribute | Specification |
|-----------|--------------|
| **Length** | 1,500–2,000 words |
| **Type** | Texte d'idees (literary excerpt OR journalistic argumentative text) |
| **Register** | Formal, intellectual |
| **Structure** | Clear argumentative structure with thesis, arguments, examples, conclusion |

**If Literary Text:**

**If Journalistic Text:**

1. Clear thesis statement (may be implicit)

### 5.4 Writing Prompt Generation Rules

#### Dossier Generation

**Input Materials:**
- 2-3 documents totaling ~1,000 words
- Common theme linking all documents
- Varied document types (press article, opinion piece, statistical data, short excerpt)
- Documents present DIFFERENT perspectives on the same theme
- At least one document presents a concrete case study or example

**Document Type Distribution:**

| Document | Type | Word Count | Purpose |
|----------|------|-----------|---------|
| Document 1 | Press article or report | 350-400 words | Factual foundation, data |
| Document 2 | Opinion piece or editorial | 350-400 words | Perspective, argumentation |
| Document 3 (optional) | Interview excerpt or case study | 200-300 words | Concrete illustration |

**Theme Selection:**
Choose from the universal theme taxonomy (see Part 3.7). Ensure theme:
- Is accessible to candidates from any academic background

- Enables both objective synthesis and personal argumentation

#### Synthese Prompt

**Instructions to generate:**
"A l'aide des documents ci-dessus, rédigez une synthèse de 220 à 240 mots présentant les éléments essentiels du dossier. Vous devez reformuler les idées en vos propres mots. N'exprimez pas d'opinion personnelle."

- Word count range: 220-240 words (strict)

- Must not include personal opinion

#### Essai Argumente Prompt

**Problematique requirements:**

**Sample problematique patterns:**

**Validation checklist:**
- Minimum 250 words
- Clear personal position
- Developed arguments with examples/justifications
- Logical structure (introduction -> arguments -> conclusion)
- Formal register
- Coherent paragraph organization

### 5.6 Automated Marking System Design

#### System Architecture Overview

The AI marking system must implement four independent evaluators:

1. **Comprehension Evaluator** (Listening & Reading)
2. **Synthesis Evaluator** (Written Production Part 1)
3. **Essay Evaluator** (Written Production Part 2)
4. **Speaking Evaluator** (Oral Production)

#### Critical System Requirements

**For Comprehension Sections (Listening & Reading):**

| Requirement | Implementation |
|-------------|---------------|
| Reformulation detection | Semantic similarity to source text; penalize near-identical phrases |
| Multiple valid answers | Accept any semantically equivalent formulation |
| Justification scoring | Award marks for reasoning quality, not just position |
| Spelling tolerance | Do not penalize minor errors that don't change meaning |
| Inference detection | Identify answers demonstrating implicit understanding |
| Keyword matching | Use as secondary criterion only (primary = semantic meaning) |

**For Synthesis Evaluation:**

| Criterion | Detection Method |
|-----------|-----------------|
| Word count (200-240) | Precise word count with strict boundaries |
| Objectivity rule | Detect personal opinion markers ("je pense", "selon moi", "il est évident que") |
| Theme identification | Compare to expected theme from source documents |
| Key information coverage | Check for presence of essential ideas from each document |
| Juxtaposed summaries | Detect document-by-document structure vs. integrated synthesis |
| Direct copying | Text similarity detection against source documents |
| Coherence evaluation | Paragraph structure, transition markers, logical flow |

**For Essay Evaluation:**

| Criterion | Detection Method |
|-----------|-----------------|
| Position clarity | Identify thesis statement and clear standpoint |
| Argument development | Check for claims + justifications + examples pattern |
| Register formality | Detect informal markers, slang, inappropriate abbreviations |
| Recipient adaptation | Identify strategies engaging the reader |
| Cohesion quality | Connector variety, paragraph transitions, logical structure |
| Word count (250+) | Precise count |
| Off-topic detection | Semantic relevance to the probleme posed |

**For Speaking Evaluation:**

| Criterion | Detection Method |
|-----------|-----------------|
| Problem statement | Transcription analysis for clear problem formulation |
| Plan structure | Detection of announced plan markers and actual structure |
| Argument quality | Claims + examples pattern in transcript |
| Interaction quality | Response relevance, debate facilitation, counter-argument handling |
| Pronunciation/fluency | Speech rate, pause patterns, intonation analysis |
| Vocabulary richness | Type-token ratio, lexical sophistication measures |
| Grammar accuracy | Error detection in spontaneous speech (with appropriate tolerance) |

#### Scoring Methodology

The AI should implement:

1. **Rubric-based scoring** with 4 performance levels: 0 / Below C1 / C1 / C1+
2. **Criterion-specific evaluation** per official grid (see Part 4)
3. **Anomaly detection:** hors-sujet, insufficient length, excessive copying
4. **Double-evaluation simulation:** Compare two independent assessments
5. **Harmonization protocol:** For divergent scores, apply official reconciliation rules

#### Scoring Algorithm by Criterion

```
function score_criterion(criterion, max_points):
    detect_anomalies()  # Apply caps first
    if anomaly_applies:
        return capped_score
    
    assess_performance_level():
        if meets_c1_plus_criteria:
            return c1_plus_score (top band)
        elif meets_c1_criteria:
            return c1_score (middle band)
        elif meets_below_c1_criteria:
            return below_c1_score (lower band)
        else:
            return 0
```

### 5.7 CEFR C1 Level Calibration Guidelines

#### Vocabulary Calibration

**C1 level vocabulary characteristics:**
- Abstract and conceptual vocabulary (not just concrete)
- Nuanced expression (multiple synonyms for same concept)
- Domain-specific terms used correctly in context
- Idiomatic expressions and collocations
- Ability to circumlocute when exact word is unknown

**Vocabulary to AVOID in generated materials (too easy):**
- Basic everyday vocabulary (manger, faire, aller, voir)
- Simple connectors (et, mais, parce que) — use sophisticated alternatives
- Repetitive vocabulary without variation

**Vocabulary to INCLUDE (C1 appropriate):**
- Abstract nouns: enjeu, paradigme, prérequis, déterminant, corollaire
- Sophisticated verbs: sous-tendre, inférer, postuler, dégager, cerner
- Advanced connectors: dans la mesure ou, partant de ce constat, en dépit de, sous réserve de
- Field-specific terminology relevant to the theme

#### Grammar Calibration

**C1 level grammar characteristics:**
- Complex subordinate clauses (subjonctif, conditionnel passe, plus-que-parfait)
- Passive voice and impersonal constructions
- Inversion and stylistic fronting
- Cleft sentences (c'est...qui/que)
- Varied sentence length and structure
- Subjunctive mood in complex expressions
- Past conditional for hypothetical scenarios

#### Discourse Calibration

**C1 discourse characteristics:**
- Implicit meaning requiring inference
- Nuanced positions (not black-and-white)
- References to broader cultural/social context
- Irony and indirect expression
- Sophisticated argumentative structure
- Adaptation to register and audience
